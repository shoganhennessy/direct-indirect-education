\section{Genetic Effects}
\label{sec:genetic-effects}
The Ed PGI, $Z_i^\text{\textcolor{ForestGreen}{Child}}$, is not randomly assigned --- individuals inherit genetic variants from their parents, creating systematic correlations with family background.
However, by isolating the random variation in genetic inheritance I recovers credible estimates of the average causal effects of the Ed PGI on education and labour market outcomes.
This section shows the empirical estimates of genetic effects.

The Ed PGI random component is a strong instrument for the Ed PGI, even when using the primary as an instrument for the secondary ($1 \to 2$, and visa versa).
\autoref{tab:genetic-firststage} summarises estimates of the genetic first-stage defined in equation \eqref{eqn:genetic-firststage}.
Columns (1) and (5) show mechanical results, equal to one by construction, as the random component is calculated from the primary and secondary Ed PGIs respectively.
Columns (2) and (4) show that the random component is a strong instrument for the Ed PGI, calculated using weights from the other sample (primary or secondary), confirming that the random component from one PGI retains predictive power for the other, independently calculated PGIs.
The fact that these cross-PGI coefficients are not equal to one indicates substantial measurement error in both PGIs, which would lead to attenuation bias in standard IV estimation.
The ORIV estimates, columns (3) and (6) explicitly addresses this measurement error by combining information from both primary and secondary first-stages.
Although ORIV estimates recover unbiased coefficients, this approach necessarily comes at the expense of greater variance.

The addition of demographic controls makes negligible differences to the correlation estimates across outcomes, indicating minimal confounding by the observed demographic factors.
However, adjusting for measurement error significantly increases the correlation estimates.
Specifically, correlations between the Ed PGI and education outcomes (education years and having a university degree) increase by approximately 30-40\%, while those for labour market outcomes (hourly wage and annual income) increase by roughly 20-40\%.
These adjusted estimates substantially exceed previously reported correlations for similar variables in earlier studies, such as \cite{papageorge2020genes}.

The IV estimates isolate causal effects by using the inferred Mendelian random component as an instrument for the Ed PGI.
Notably, these IV estimates are systematically lower than their corresponding OLS correlations.
This discrepancy implies that growing up in a family environment shaped by parents with higher Ed PGI values independently and positively influences children's educational attainment and labour market outcomes, beyond the direct genetic influences captured by the Ed PGI.

The IV estimates confirm meaningful average causal effects of the Ed PGI. For example, a one-standard-deviation increase in the Ed PGI causally leads to an additional 0.74 years of education and about a 6.1\% increase in occupation coded hourly wages, according to the ORIV estimates.
Adjusting for measurement error notably affects these causal estimates, increasing them by roughly 30-40\% when moving from standard IV (Column 4) to ORIV specifications (Column 6), validating the importance of addressing measurement error.
These causal estimates are meaningfully larger than those recently presented by \cite{carvalho2024genetics}, who analysed the same data but did not account for measurement error.

Correcting for measurement error comes at a cost: the standard errors in ORIV specifications are noticeably larger than those in the unadjusted IV columns. 
This increase reflects proper acknowledgment of statistical uncertainty in the GWAS weights used to construct the Ed PGI.
Prior studies reporting smaller standard errors do not account for this additional uncertainty, and thus substantially overstate the precision and reliability of their correlation or causal effect estimates.
