\section{Genetic Effects}
\label{sec:genetic-effects}
The Ed PGI, $Z_i^\text{\textcolor{ForestGreen}{Child}}$, is not randomly assigned—individuals inherit genetic variants from their parents, creating systematic correlations with family background.
However, by isolating the random variation in genetic inheritance I construct a formula instrument that recovers credible estimates of the average causal effects of the Ed PGI on education and labour market outcomes.
This section explicitly defines the target causal parameters, clearly states the identifying assumptions, and provides the corresponding estimating equations.

\subsection{Identification Strategy}
Formally, let $D_i(z')$ denote the potential education outcome (either measured by education years or higher education attendance) for individual $i$, under the hypothetical alternative realisation that $i$ had Ed PGI value $Z_i^\text{\textcolor{ForestGreen}{Child}} = z'$.
Similarly, define $Y_i(z', D_i(z'))$ as the potential labour market outcome (occupation-coded hourly wages or annual income) in the same hypothetical case of $i$ having $Z_i^\text{\textcolor{ForestGreen}{Child}} = z'$.
Note that notation includes $D_i(z')$ entering labour market outcome, because $z'$ affects education, which may go on to affect labour market outcomes --- this issue is further explored in \autoref{sec:direct}.

Observed education and wages/income data are a sum of their respective potential outcomes.
\[ D_i = \sum_{z'}
    \indicator{Z_i^\text{\textcolor{ForestGreen}{Child}} = z'} D_i(z'),
    \quad Y_i = \sum_{z'} 
    \indicator{Z_i^\text{\textcolor{ForestGreen}{Child}} = z'} Y_i(z', D_i(z')).
\]

\noindent
\textbf{Target Parameters.}
This study aims to estimate the average causal effect of having a higher Ed PGI, which is summarised by the following average partial effects:
\[ \partialdiff{z'} \E{D_i(z')}, \quad \partialdiff{z'} \E{Y_i(z', D_i(z'))}. \]
The first average partial effect represents the average effect of a one standard deviation change in the Ed PGI on education, and the second on labour market wages or income.

The Ed PGI, however, is neither randomly assigned nor directly manipulatable, so that we may not observe $D_i(z')$ nor $Y_i(z', D_i(z'))$ for different values of $z'$ for the same individual, and causal effects are not identifiable without further assumptions (i.e., the fundamental problem of causal inference).
Thus, I rely on random variation coming from the random component of the Ed PGI to infer causal effects.

\noindent
\textbf{Identifying Assumptions.}
I use the random component of the Ed PGI, $Z_i^\text{\textcolor{red}{Random}}$, as an instrument for the Ed PGI, $Z_i^\text{\textcolor{ForestGreen}{Child}}$.
This requires a number of assumptions to recover valid average causal effects.
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
    \item Instrument Relevance and Independence: The random component of the Ed PGI strongly predicts the individual's Ed PGI, and is unrelated to potential outcomes.
    \item Instrument Monotonicity: 
    A higher value of the random component (weakly) increases the Ed PGI, with probability 1.
    \item Exclusion Restriction:
    The random genetic component, $Z_i^\text{\textcolor{red}{Random}}$, affects the potential outcomes only via the Ed PGI, $Z_i^\text{\textcolor{ForestGreen}{Child}}$.
\end{enumerate}

The three identifying assumptions are justified by the biology of genetic inheritance and the design of the empirical strategy.
The relevance and independence assumption (1) holds because the random genetic component arises solely from Mendelian inheritance, ensuring both predictive strength (see \autoref{tab:genetic-firststage}) and independence from unobserved confounding conditional on parental genetics and observed covariates.
The monotonicity assumption (2) is satisfied because inheriting a higher random genetic component can only additively increase the Ed PGI, never reducing it.
Indeed, the random component mechanically changes the Ed PGI for anyone who receives a higher random component, so that everyone is a complier, too.
Finally, the exclusion restriction (3) is credible given that genetic variants used in the random component influence socioeconomic outcomes only through their aggregation into the Ed PGI, by construction.

\noindent
\textbf{Estimation Strategy.}
I estimate the average partial effects with a regression specification as follows, where $\vec X_i$ is a vector of control variables described previously.
\begin{align}
    \label{eqn:parametric-firststage}
    D_i &= \phi + \pi Z_i^\text{\textcolor{ForestGreen}{Child}}
        + \vec \zeta' \vec X_i + \eta_i,  \\
    \label{eqn:parametric-secondstage}
    Y_i &= \alpha + \beta Z_i^\text{\textcolor{ForestGreen}{Child}}
        + \vec \varphi' \vec X_i + \varepsilon_i.
\end{align}
$\pi$ represents the average effect of the Ed PGI on the education variables, and $\beta$ similarly on wages and annual income (measured in $\log$ values).
I use the Ed PGI random component, $\tilde Z_i^\text{\textcolor{red}{Random}}$, to instrument for the Ed PGI in the IV specifications, using the ORIV method (with Ed PGI calculated using two independent weights) where mentioned.
The IV first-stage is shown in \autoref{eqn:genetic-firststage}, with the same set of control variables as both estimating equations above.

\noindent
\textbf{Limitations.}
The OLS and IV estimates place greater weight on individuals with larger deviations in the random component, $\tilde Z_i^\text{\textcolor{red}{Random}}$, which might not represent effects for the entire population --- potentially limiting external validity.
Additionally, despite independent Mendelian inheritance, the random genetic component remains modestly correlated with other PGIs.
While I control for these other PGI values, these correlations further constrain generalisability beyond the analysis sample, by implying overlap with the Ed PGI and these other PGIs.
Finally, the linear specifications employed (OLS and ORIV) yield weighted averages of individual-level causal effects.
This linear specification might not fully reflect heterogeneity in causal effects across the PGI distribution, or provide weights for the average causal effects representative for the entire UKB population --- see \cite{blandhol2022tsls} for further details.
