\section{Data and Genetic Context}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{UK Biobank (UKB)}
\label{sec:data-UKB}

The UK Biobank (UKB) is a large-scale prospective cohort study comprising approximately half a million members of the British public, recruited between 2006 and 2010.
Letters were sent to a random sample of addresses in the National Health Service registry, and the final UKB data represent voluntary respondents.\footnote{
    The UKB may not be representative of the entire UK population, as it only represents willing respondents.
    As such, the findings of this paper should only be taken as internally valid among the UKB respondents, and the sibling analysis sample described.
}
Participants, aged 40-69 years at recruitment, underwent comprehensive baseline assessments, asked many questions for socioeconomic information either by interview, automated survey answers, or follow-up sessions.
Key socioeconomic variables include educational attainment (measured in education qualifications), household income brackets, employment status, and industry of employment.
% Participants also provided saliva samples, to collect DNA for genetic measures.
See \cite{sudlow2015uk} for a full description of the UKB organisation and data.

The UKB collected detailed information on educational qualifications, including completion of primary school, GCSEs (typically completed at age 16), A-Levels (completed at age 18), and completion of an higher education degree.
I convert these qualifications into education years based on each participant's highest reported qualification, following the International Standard Classification of Education.\footnote{
    As noted by \cite{okbay2022polygenic}, British vocational degrees inconsistently coded by the International Standard Classification of Education.
    I follow the literature by assigning education years as the age they reported leaving full-time education minus five, among those with a vocational degree as their highest qualification.
}
While the UKB collected household income data, this information was recorded only in categorical bins, limiting its utility for continuous analysis.
Following \cite{kweon2025associations}, I construct an individual-level income measure using participants' 4-digit Standard Occupational Classification (SOC) codes.
Specifically, I impute occupation-based wages by estimating hourly earnings for each SOC category using data from the British Annual Survey of Hours and Earnings, conditional on age, gender, and year.
This approach provides a more granular and continuous measure of economic status than the categorical household income variable (see \appendixref{appendix:wage-impute} for details on the imputation procedure).
I create the annual occupation income by multiplying this by the number of hours they reported working, times 52 weeks in the year.
For example, this procedure gives a female participant, age 58 in 2010 with SOC 2314 (i.e., a secondary education teacher by profession), an occupation hourly wage of $\pounds 29.20$ and an annual income of $\pounds 68,476$ for her 45 hours worked each week (adjusted to 2024 \pounds GBP by CPI-H).

For the purposes of this study, the primary analysis sample is restricted to those with at least one family member, sibling or parent, also enrolled in the UKB, and non-missing data in occupation code plus education qualifications.
The UKB does not personally identify participants, nor their family members, so I follow the genetic literature by identifying family clusters by measures of genetic similarity.\footnote{
    The design of the UKB allows for a larger number of family clusters than would be expected from a random sample of the British population.
    This is because study enrolment letters were sent to households, and the household head (or to whomever the letter was addressed) could optionally enrol their family members in addition to themselves.
    \appendixref{appendix:identifying-families} describes the family cluster identification procedure in detail, which has been used in previous social science genetic studies (see e.g., \citealt{muslimova2025environment}). 
}
The presence of multiple family members within the dataset provides a unique opportunity to control for shared genetic factors that might otherwise bias estimates in standard cross-sectional analyses.

The analysis sample is also restricted to individuals who report to have primarily European ancestry.
The genetic measures used in this paper have generally been constructing from primarily white, European descended populations, and have no predictive power out of sample, so are only relevant in this subsample.\footnote{
    It is an unfortunate fact that systematic genetic measures, such as those considered in this paper, have only been constructed from Biobank samples where the vast majority are white and/or European descended; see \cite{martin2017human} for a further discussion.
    Note that this implies this paper has nothing to say about race; see \autoref{sec:ethics}.
}
The only notable differences between the analysis subsample and the entire UKB participant sample is that the analysis sample has more siblings, and is entirely European descended; no other variables are significantly different for the analysis subsample.
\autoref{tab:ukb-summary} summarises the UKB data, for the analysis sample and among the entire UKB sample.
\autoref{fig:ukb-edyears-hist} summarises the correlation between education years and occupation coded income.

\subsection{Education PolyGenic Index (Ed PGI)}
\label{sec:ed-pgi}
UKB participants also provided saliva samples that were subsequently genotyped using high-density arrays containing over 800,000 genetic markers, producing standardised genetic data files for statistical analysis of the relationship between genetics and social outcomes.

While genetic data provide rich information about individual variation, the relationship between specific genes and social outcomes like education is extraordinarily complex.
The biological pathways from DNA to observable traits remain largely opaque --- the exact relationship between a single gene, its resulting protein (or its moderating role), and the effects of those proteins are only known for a small number of genes in the human genome.
For example, a single gene (and its recessive variant) is known to lead to a protein which does not break down the by-products of alcohol digestion; individuals with this gene generally do not drink alcohol at all because of the simple limit on digestion \citep{millwood2023alcohol}.
Complex social behaviours, on the other hand, have no single genes with a known biological pathway, so cannot be summarised in a single gene, nor single biological pathway.
Instead complex social behaviours are a result on thousands of genes, and their interactions with the environment --- they are polygenic.

Rather than attempting to trace all of these intricate biological mechanisms, researchers have developed PolyGenic Indices (PGIs) as dimensional reduction tools that summarise the statistical relationship between thousands of genetic variants and outcomes of interest.\footnote{
    See \cite{uffelmann2021genome} for a full explanation of how a GWAS works, and the approach to calculating PGIs.
}
This aggregation approach is particularly valuable because individual genetic variants typically have tiny effects on complex traits like educational attainment --- often explaining less than 0.01\% of the variation.
However, by combining information across hundreds of thousands of variants, each contributing a small effect, PGIs can capture meaningful portions of the genetic contribution to these outcomes.
These indices are fundamentally correlational measures: they capture statistical associations between genetic variants and outcomes observed in large samples, not well understood biological pathways, and without a clear case for causal relationships.
The weights used to construct PGIs reflect population-level correlations that may arise through various channels, including biological effects, gene-environment correlations, and population stratification.

The Education PGI (Ed PGI) is a summary measure that aggregates genetic information across thousands of genetic variants into a single index predictive of educational attainment.
The construction of the Ed PGI relies on weights derived from a Genome-Wide Association Study (GWAS), which analyse the correlation between genetic variants and education years.
In a GWAS, researchers test the correlation between each Single Nucleotide Polymorphism (SNP) --- a one-letter change in the DNA sequence at a specific location --- and the outcome of interest, in this case education years.
I use the weights calculated in the GWAS conducted by \cite{okbay2022polygenic}, which used a sample of over 3 million individuals of European ancestry, primarily from 23\&Me data.

The Ed PGI is defined as the weighted sum of whether individual $i$ has the relevant SNP at each DNA location $j$,
\[ Z_i = \sum_{j = 1}^{J} w_j x_{i,j} \]
where $w_j$ are the Ed PGI weights (provided in the supplementary data to \citealt{okbay2022polygenic}), and $x_{i,j}$ is an indicator of whether individual $i$ has the relevant SNP at DNA location $j$.\footnote{
    Every person has two copies of each chromosome, so that $x_{i,j}$ takes values 0, 1, 2 corresponding to how many copies of the SNP $i$ has.
    The genetics literature has generally accepted that complex social behaviours are explained by additive effects (see \citealt{hill2008data}), and \cite{okbay2022polygenic} found little evidence for dominance in the Ed PGI SNPs, so that the Ed PGI makes sense as a weighted sum.
}
The \cite{okbay2022polygenic} GWAS found that over 8,000 individual SNPs were significant in predicting education years, after passing a battery of biostatistical tests and finding no evidence for significant interactions.
The resulting Ed PGI is standardised to have mean zero and standard deviation one within the entire sample, making it interpretable as the number of standard deviations from the population mean.
The Ed PGI captures approximately 12-15\% of the variation in educational attainment, making it one of the most predictive PGIs for any complex behavioural trait to date.

The Ed PGI is highly correlated with education years, as it is constructed to be so.
A one standard deviation increase in the Ed PGI is associated with 0.93 extra education years (standard error 0.02), with an $R^2$ value of 0.10 for the raw regression of education years on Ed PGI.
It is roughly normally distributed, with standard deviation normalised to 1.
It is not surprising that the Ed PGI is normally distributed; it is the weighted sum of thousands of binary variables, among a few thousand individuals, so we should expect the central limit theorem to apply.
Additionally, it is not surprising that the Ed PGI is correlated with education years; it was literally constructed to be so.
The main take-away is that the correlation is strong, and has meaningful predictive power for education years.
If education had no relationship with genetic variants, then we may expect the correlation would be indistinguishable from zero.

The Ed PGI is not the only PGI that can be constructed.
Following the same methodology, I construct additional PGIs for six health-related traits that may confound the relationship between genetics and socioeconomic outcomes: asthma, bipolar disorder, Body Mass Index (BMI), height, schizophrenia, and type 2 diabetes.
Each PGI is calculated using the same approach as the Ed PGI --- taking weights from a published GWAS, conducted on independent populations, and computing weighted sums of the relevant genetic variants in the UKB data.
These other PGIs serve as control variables in my analysis for two reasons.
First, genetic variants often exhibit pleiotropy, meaning a single variant can influence multiple traits simultaneously.
For instance, genetic variants associated with education might also affect other factors (e.g., mental health conditions), which could independently influence labour market outcomes.
Second, systematic differences in gene frequencies across ancestry groups can create spurious correlations between any PGI and outcomes if health conditions vary across these groups for unrelated reasons.

\subsection{Imputing Parental PGIs from Siblings}
\label{sec:pgi-impute}
It would be ideal if the UKB contained genetic information for the parents of every UKB participant.
While there are 6,239 participants with a parent also included in the UKB, there are only 72 with both parents.
To overcome this missing data problem, I use siblings' genetic data to impute the mean parental PGIs from the sample of \input{../text/sections/tables/ukb-analysis-count.txt} participants with at least one sibling also in the UKB --- the sibling analysis sample described above.

I use the \cite{young2022mendelian} procedure, to impute mean parental PGI values using genetic data on participants' siblings.
To briefly summarise how this works, consider that each person inherits exactly half their DNA from their mother and half from their father.
When we observe two siblings, we can partially reconstruct their parents' genetic information because siblings both inherit their DNA from parents.
Consider observing two different samples from the same underlying distribution --- the parents' genetics.
At some genetic locations, siblings inherited the same variant from a parent (like both getting mum's brown-eye variant), whilst at others they inherited different variants (one got mum's brown-eye variant, the other her blue-eye variant).
By comparing siblings' genetics across thousands of genetic variants, we can recover about 75\% of their parents' combined genetic information --- enough to construct a reasonably accurate parental PGI.

    \footnotesize
    \textbf{Note}:
    This figure illustrates the logic of imputing parental genotypes from sibling data at a single SNP.
    At each SNP, individuals have a genotype of 0, 1, or 2, representing the number of copies of a particular variant inherited from their parents.
    A child, and their sibling, jointly imply the possible options for the parents.
    The first option comes from the fact that child and sibling configuration of $\{1,0\}$ is only possible with the configuration of parents of $\{0,1\}, \{1,0\}$ or $\{1,1\}$.
    With a parent configuration of $\{0,1\}$ or $\{1,0\}$, there are 4 possible realisations with a child and a sibling taking $\{1,0\}$, and 3 for parental configuration of $\{1,1\}$.
    The second option is only possible with one parent configuration, so their is no uncertainty in what the parent values were.
    This gives the relevant probabilities as a result of this combinatorial mixing, which is also shown in \autoref{fig:mendelian-indep}.
\end{figure}

\autoref{fig:pgi-imputing} visualises the logic behind imputing parental SNP values based on an individual's, and their sibling(s)'s, genetic data.
In the middle panel, the individual has genotype 1 and their sibling has genotype 0.
Since the individual must have inherited their 1 variant from one parent and their 0 variant from the other, while the sibling inherited two 0 variants, we can infer that one parent has genotype 0 (contributing 0 to both children) and the other has genotype 1 (contributing 1 to the individual and 0 to the sibling).
The right panel shows an alternative configuration yielding the same sibling genotypes.
When parental genotypes cannot be uniquely determined, the imputation uses the expected values based on observed variants and population frequencies.
By applying this logic across hundreds of thousands of SNPs and accounting for segments of DNA shared identically by descent between siblings, we can accurately reconstruct parental PGIs despite never observing the parents directly.

The imputation procedure performs well in the UKB analysis sample.
Following the \cite{young2022mendelian} validation approach, I verify the accuracy by examining the small subset of families where parental genotypes are actually observed.
The imputed parental PGIs show a correlation of over [X] with true parental PGIs, and regression coefficients are statistically indistinguishable from 1, confirming the imputation is unbiased.
\appendixref{appendix:pgi-impute} describes the full technical detail behind the imputation procedure, and this validation exercise.
This high accuracy means I can confidently use the imputed parental PGIs in place of directly observed parental PGI values.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:ethics}
It is crucial to emphasise that the genetic measures in this study should not be misinterpreted as evidence of genetic determinism.
Having a higher Ed PGI does not destine someone to higher education or income; it merely represents only a small statistical tendency, not fate.
These tendencies are fundamentally shaped by social policies, institutional structures, and cultural contexts that vary across time and place.
Just as eyesight correcting glasses  can eliminate the practical consequences of genetic variation in eyesight, education and economic policies can modify or eliminate the consequences of genetic variation in traits relevant to socioeconomic outcomes.

Furthermore, this research provides no support whatsoever for ideas about ``natural'' social hierarchies or genetic justifications for inequality.
In addition, genetic associations identified within one ancestry group cannot be meaningfully compared across groups due to different genetic architectures, environmental contexts, and historical experiences.
Any claim using the measures consider in this paper for inference across racial groups, or different racial groups would be both scientifically invalid, and ethically deficient.
The appropriate interpretation of these results is that they highlight how both social and genetic  factors influence socioeconomic outcomes, which should inspire greater solidarity and support for policies that promote equality of opportunity rather than acceptance of inequality as natural or inevitable.
