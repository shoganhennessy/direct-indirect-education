\section{Direct and Indirect Effects}
\label{sec:direct}
The Ed PGI exhibits clear labour-market effects in the data.
IV estimates that isolate the random component of genetic inheritance indicate that a one-standard-deviation increase in the Ed PGI causally raises education and improves labour earnings: the preferred ORIV specifications imply roughly 0.74 additional years of education and about a 6.1\% increase in occupation-coded hourly wages per s.d. of the Ed PGI.
These causal effects exceed earlier reports that did not correct for measurement error in PGI weights, consistent with the stacked/ORIV adjustments materially inflating both education and earnings relationships once attenuation is addressed.

The size and robustness of these labour-market effects has led to a common interpretation that at least part of the Ed PGI's influence must be direct, operating through traits such as intelligence rather than exclusively through education.
This view is often motivated by the fact that the Ed PGI predicts fluid-intelligence scores and by results that show earnings associations even when conditioning on education \citep{carvalho2024genetics}.
Such claims appear, for example, in work that argues the Ed PGI captures skills related to financial literacy and other non-educational attributes \citep{barth2020genetic}.

This section examines those claims with a Causal Mediation (CM) framework to separate direct genetic effects from indirect effects via take-up and returns to education.
The direct and indirect average partial effects are defined as follows, where $Y_i$ is again a measure of labour market wages or income, and $D_i$ a measure of education (education years or a binary measure for an education qualification).
\[ \text{Direct Effect } = \partialdiff{z'}
    \E{Y_i(z', D_i(Z_i^\text{\textcolor{ForestGreen}{Child}}))}, \;\;\;\; 
    \text{Indirect Effect } = \partialdiff{z'}
    \E{Y_i(Z_i^\text{\textcolor{ForestGreen}{Child}}, D_i(z'))}. \]

The direct effect corresponds to the average partial effect of the Ed PGI on wages or income while holding the education channel $D_i$ fixed; the indirect effect captures the average partial effect that operates through the education response $D_i(z')$.
This decomposition maps to the empirical question raised above: ``do education-linked genes affect earnings directly, or mainly by shifting the education margin?''

Valid inference on CM effects requires valid causal estimates of returns to education, to avoid selection bias in CM estimates and contaminated inference (see \citealt{hogan2025causal}).

Education is not randomly assigned, so I proceed on two complementary avenues for credible returns to education estimates: (i) exploiting the 1972 British Minimum School-Leaving Age (MSLA) reform, which induces an extra year of schooling to estimate education returns for MLSA compliers; and (ii) sibling fixed-effects estimates, which offer conservative, design-consistent benchmarks within the UKB sibling analysis sample.
These ingredients are used below to interpret the mediated component of the Ed PGI's labour-market effect, and to assess the scope for a direct effect.

Also note that one cannot use the ORIV to purge measurement error in this analysis, as it makes everything too imprecise for meaningful inference.
So this part is using estimates that suffer from measurement error; if one assumes that average direct and indirect effects suffer equally from  attenuation bias, then the proportion going through the indirect education channel is representative.

\subsection{Minimum School Leaving Age (MSLA)}
In 1972, the UK increased the Minimum School Leaving Age (MSLA) by one year, from age 15 to 16.
The change took effect at the start of the 1972-73 school year, and applied to those who were 15 or younger on 1 September 1972, i.e., those born on or after 1 September 1957.
The reform therefore induced extra school for individuals born around date 1 September 1957.
At the end of the school year which became compulsory, British school children sit exams for the GCSE qualification, so the reform pushed a larger share of the UKB cohorts to remain in school to 16 and to complete these qualifications.
The 1972 MLSA change has been previously used for labour market returns to education \citep{oreopoulos2006estimating} and health returns to education \citep{clark2013effect}.
The UKB population overlaps well with the birth date cut-off, and so has been used in a series of papers for estimating returns to education in the UKB \citep{barcellos2021effect,barcellos2023distributional}.

The analysis treats exact birth month  as the running variable in a fuzzy regression discontinuity design (RDD), using the indicator for being born September 1957 and after as an instrument.
Because not all those eligible necessarily stayed beyond 15, the post-cutoff indicator instruments (i) GCSE completion, in specifications focussed on credentials, (ii) education years, where mentioned.
I estimate the following RDD equation, to estimate the effect of the MSLA change on education,
\[ D_i = l_0(b_i) + \indicator{1957.75 \leq b_i} + l_1(b_i) + \epsilon_i \]
where $b_i$ represents individual $i$'s birth year plus birth month divided by 12 (so that 1957.75 represents born in September 1957), and $l_0, l_1$ are control trends either side of the cut-off.

I estimate the RDD equation with triangular kernel weights (weighting closer to the cut-off), and in two main specifications --- one with a linear trend before and after the cutoff and a bandwidth of 5 years, and another with a polynomial in birth month, and a bandwidth of 10 years (following \citealt{barcellos2021effect}).
The polynomial specification thus has more observations, as it includes people who are born further before or after the MSLA change.

A practical constraint concerns sample size around the 1 September 1957 threshold.
The sibling subsample is too small to deliver precise RD estimates for years of education: there are too few sibling observations in the immediate neighbourhood of the cutoff to identify a stable first-stage effect on education.
For the RD analysis, the estimation therefore uses the broader UK Biobank cohort --- restricting to births within 5 or 10 years of the cutoff --- to increase the density of observations close to the threshold while preserving local comparability.
This design yields substantially more information at the policy margin and improves power for both the discontinuity in schooling and the corresponding fuzzy RD/IV estimates.
All RD results are reported with both specifications, showing that conclusions are unchanged across the 5 and 10 year windows; SEs are clustered two-way at the month $+$ birth year level.
\autoref{fig:rosla} shows the effect of the MSLA change on GCSE completion rate and mean education years, using a squared polynomial either side of the cut-off and a bandwidth of 10 years before and after September 1975.

The policy generated a clear discontinuity in schooling at the date-of-birth cutoff: the share completing the GCSE qualifications rose after for students born in September 1957 and afterwards, with a corresponding (smaller) increase in mean education years
These first-stage effects are visible in UK Biobank and are consistent with nationally representative evidence on the same reform --- and previous research that estimate this same cut-off.

Panel A of \autoref{tab:msla-iv} documents clear discontinuities at the September 1957 cutoff.
In the narrower bandwidth with linear cohort trends (columns 1 and 3), the post-cutoff indicator increases the probability of completing GCSE/O-level by 5.3 p.p. (s.e. 0.2 p.p.) and raises education years by 0.122 (s.e. 0.016) years. In the wider bandwidth with quadratic trends (columns 2 and 4), the corresponding effects are 6.0 p.p. (0.7 p.p.) and 0.201 (0.033) years. 
Reduced-form impacts on log hourly wages and log annual income are mechanically small and positive—0.006--0.011 and 0.001--0.014 log points, respectively—consistent with modest average shifts in schooling.
IV relevance is very strong for the education outcomes (e.g., F = 1049, 79 for GCSE completion; 56, 37 for education years).

Using the post-cutoff indicator as an instrument yields estimates for labour market returns to education among MLSA compliers on the leaving-age margin, shown in Panel B of \autoref{tab:msla-iv}.
For log hourly wages, OLS estimates imply 6.1\% per additional year of education across both bandwidths, and the MLSA IV estimates lie in the 4.5--5.5\% range.
For log annual income, OLS returns are 6.5--6.6\%, while the IV range is 1.2--7.1\% across specifications.
For labour market returns to to GCSE completion rate, OLS associations are 0.258--0.272 log points and the IV estimates 0.104--0.231.
Overall, the IV returns are of similar order of magnitude to their OLS counterparts, supporting the use of these MLSA-anchored LATEs as credible benchmarks for the mediation exercise.
